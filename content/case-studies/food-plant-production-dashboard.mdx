## The problem in detail

Three production lines. Each line had a PLC logging runtime data. That data went into a SCADA system that nobody read in real time — it was used for post-shift reporting.

Shift supervisors kept their own tallies. At the end of each shift, they filled in a spreadsheet. Someone collated those spreadsheets and sent a summary to management by 8am.

By the time management saw a problem, the problem was 12 hours old.

## What we built

### Data collection layer

An MQTT broker sits at the edge, subscribed to event topics from each PLC. Every machine start, stop, fault, and product count publishes a message. A lightweight Node.js consumer normalises those messages and writes to a time-series-friendly PostgreSQL schema.

No polling. No batch jobs. Events flow as they happen.

### The dashboard

A Next.js application renders live data for each line: OEE broken down into availability, performance, and quality; a downtime log with duration and reason codes; and a shift summary that updates every 30 seconds.

Supervisors enter downtime reason codes from the floor using a tablet-optimised input screen. That context appears in the dashboard immediately — no separate system, no duplicate entry.

### Alerting

When a line goes down, a Slack message fires within 90 seconds. When OEE drops below threshold, the shift supervisor gets a WhatsApp alert. No email — nobody reads email on the floor.

## What changed

The first week the dashboard went live, the operations manager identified a recurring 20-minute stoppage on Line 2 that had been happening three times a week for months. It had never surfaced in the spreadsheet reports because it was spread across shifts and nobody had connected the pattern.

That stoppage is now on the maintenance schedule.
